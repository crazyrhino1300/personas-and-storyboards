Evaluating the Ratty Drink Machine

As an avid patron of Brown's ~beloved~ Sharpe refectory (Ratty), it's no surprise that patrons have mixed feelings about the Ratty's food; I myself am often conflicted. Hit-or-miss is the adjective that best describes the Ratty; but I've always wondered what people's opinions were about the drink experience.

This sketch shows a rough idea of what the interface looks like. The screen contains many circular buttons to choose a beverage, and the right lever under the screen dispenses the beverage.

In order to better understand the experience of Ratty beverage drinkers, I developed a series of questions to interview random Ratty patrons with, and I ultimately developed two personas and storyboard based on these interviews.

Part 1: Preparation

Upon observing the drink machine, there were two points of interaction that I wanted to learn more about: the landing page to select a drink, and the lever/space under the actual dispenser. I thought that, given the layout of the drink choices on the landing page, that different users would interact differently with the screen in order to facilitate their experience, so I designed the following questions to investigate this:

  • Prepared Questions:
    1. What jumps out at you when you first approach the drink machine?
    2. Does the interface of the drink machine make it easy to get your drink?
    3. What are some limitations that you experience in the using the drink despenser as it's currently designed?
    4. What vessel do you typically use to contain the drink that you get from the drink machine?
    5. If your drink was dispensed by both the milk dispenser and the current drink dispenser, which machine would you use?

Part 2: Recording Observations

While interviewing Ratty patrons, I identified three key users that exemplified what I thought to be defining archetypical behaviors and thoughts.

    From a water drinker:
  • First impression: many many bubbles and circles to press on the landing screen.
  • It was difficult to navigate the drink selection page if you weren't already familiar with the layout.
  • The number of buttons presented was also overwhelming, which deterred this user from exploring other possible drinks.
  • This user was a water drinker, so her main vessel of choice was usually either the provided the cups, but also her waterbottle.
  • This user also felt that the space between the dispenser and the ice tray was too small, as it often didn't allow bottles to fit under the dispenser.
    From a soda Drinker
  • The first thing that jumps out are the contrasting colors, both on the actual machine and the screen of the machine.
  • This user felt that the organization of the drink choices made it easy to navigate and select a drink.
    • Drinks of the same category were clustered together, which made it easy to filter what the user wanted and didn't want.
  • No true limitations, except potentially cumbersome navigation of sub-flavors for certain sodas.
    • This implementation is the best of all options, as displaying all options would clutter the landing page.
  • This user often used a cup to get his beverages.
  • If this user could use a simpler UI, like the milk machine, to get the same drink, he would.
    From an infrequent user:
  • First impressions: all central choices were from Coca-Cola subsidiary companies. Everything else was pushed to the side.
    • Noted that organization in rows could have been a better option.
  • Larger subdivision by juice, soda, etc. makes it easy to filter type of drink, but within subdivisions, more organization is needed.
    • Messy organization within subdivions makes it more difficult to try new drinks.
  • Current limitations include the organization of the drink choices, but also how it's limited to only syrup-cocktail drinks.
  • This user primarily uses a 40 ounce water-bottle with the machine. His waterbottle frequently does not fit under the machine, and it makes refilling the water bottle hasslesome.
  • He would choose the ratty machine because he likes having an active UI; feels that it improves experience.

Part 3: Personas

Description

This Qristen Quahog's empathy map; She represents the archetypical behavior of a drink dispenser-user who typically gets soda or a non-water beverage at the dispenser. Qristen faces long lines and delays when trying to get a drink with her meal. As a soda drinker, Qristen feels that the interface is easy to use, since she is accustomed to drinking Dr. Pepper everyday; she is extremely proficient in navigating the interface. The only slight limitation was that it took a little more effort to reach sub-flavors of certain sodas, but she feels that there are no better alternatives, so she is content with this implementation.

Qristen represents the archetypical soda-drinker at the Ratty. Based on observation, soda drinkers are much more attuned to the layout of the soda and non-water beverage layout of the machine, which makes it much easier for these users to use the machine, as shown in Qristen's empathy map. From observation, soda drinkers are also much more likely to use cups as their containing vessel, which is reflected in Qristen's empathy map as well.

Description

This is Carter Providence's empathy map; he represents the archetypical behavior of a drink dispenser-user who typically gets water at the dispenser. Carter's main challenges include indecision and pressure to make a decision. As Carter experiences, the drink machine's interface has many options for non-water beverages, and as someone who doesn't typically drink non-water beverages, browsing these other beverages often feels pressure-some, especially when there's a line forming. Carter's main challenge in using the machine lies in the relatively scattered organization of the drink choices and its difficult browsing for less-familiar users. Carter also likes to fill up his water bottle at the drink dispenser after his meals. Another main challenge with this interface that it only allows for vessels up to a certain height to fit under the machine, so he finds it difficult to fill up his water bottle without either making a mess or having to use another cup to transfer water into his bottle.

Carter represents users who often use the drink machine just for water, or typically don't use the machine at all. Upon interviewing non-soda drinkers, they express discontentment with how difficult the machine can be to navigate when trying to try a new drink, especially during peak hours. These non-soda drinkers also tend to carry larger waterbottles, which they often face difficulty with when trying to refill them at the drink dispenser.

Part 4: Storyboards

This storyboard illustrates how Carter would interact with the Ratty drink machine. (please use the + feature in the pdf to magnify the dialogue.)

Final conclusions: In general, for soda-drinkers, the machine seems to be well designed. Generally easy to navigate if you know what you want. But for users who typically drink water and aren't used to navigating the system, it can be difficult to use sometimes, especially under pressure. As such, a redesign of this machine would ideally increase the usability for those users who aren't as familiar with sodas and non-water beverages.
